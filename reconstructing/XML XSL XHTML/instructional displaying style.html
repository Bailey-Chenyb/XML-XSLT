Is this what you are expecting?

Aster is a flower that looks similar to daisies because of its bright yellow, round center that is surrounded by colorful petals. Aster is a perennial, which means it comes back year after year. It grows in many different colors with white, blue, pink and purple being the most common. Aster flowers can reach up to 8 feet tall depending on the variety, and the flowers will attract butterflies to the garden

Planting Steps:

1. Condition: general
Begin Aster seeds indoors six weeks before the last frost.

2. Condition: general
Plant two seeds 1/8 deep in a smallpot with a sterile potting mix.

3. Condition: general
Make sure the soil is thoroughly wateredbafter planting the seeds.

4. Condition: general

5. Condition: general

Growing Steps:

Condition 1: general
Step 1. Once seedlings have their second set of leaves, thin them so only the strongest one remains in each pot.

Step 2. Once seedlings are 6 inches tall, transplant them outdoors.

Step 3. Space them 18 inches apart. Aster grows best in full sun or partial shade.

Condition 2: containers-grown
Step 1. In addition to growing well in a flower bed, Aster also grows well in containers as a potted plant. Instead of transplanting seedlings to a flower bed, they can also be transplanted to a 12-inch diameter pot outside.

Step 2. After transplanting, add mulch around the plants to keep the soil cool and retain moisture.

Condition 3: general
Step 1. Aster flowers require 1 inch of water per week.

Condition 4: deadheading
Step 1. They grow better when trimmed lightly and when their dead flowers are deadheaded. Deadheading is done by pinching off the dead blooms.

Condition 5: taller variaty
Step 1. Also, with taller varieties of the plant, cut back any stems that begin to grow leggy.

Step 2. Every three years, divide the Aster plants to maintain the flower quality and growth.

Harvest Steps:

1. To harvest, cut the stem low to the ground and check for insects that may be hiding in the stem, flowers or leaves.

2. Bring the cuttings indoors and place in water immediately.

Pictures of Cosmos

Is this what you are expecting?

Brief Introduction:

Cosmos are often seen in wildflower mixes, and they have bright colors that make good borders or as cut flowers. Cosmos have many different varieties which reach different heights and have an assortment of colors. Cosmos can appear in single or double blooms and are easy to care for. The flowers can reach up to 4 feet high but other varieties only reach 1 foot high. Cosmos are a member of the aster family and have daisy-likeflowers. The flowers will attract butterflies to the garden.

Planting Steps:

1. Condition: general
Cosmos grow best in soil that has not been amended, so do not amend the soil prior to planting.

2. Condition: general
The flowers are usually grown from seed, and they are typically directly sown into the outdoor garden.

3. Condition: general
Scatter the seeds around the planting area and cover lightly with soil, no more than 1⁄4 inch.

4. Condition: mix varieties
When deciding where to plant Cosmos, be sure to plant taller varieties in the back of the garden bed so they don’t shade other Cosmos or flowers growing there. Dwarf varieties should be planted as a border along garden beds. It will take up to seven weeks for Cosmos to bloom if planted from seed.

5. Condition: early start
To start early, plant the seeds indoors six weeks before the last frost with a seed-starting mix.

Growing Steps:

Condition 1: indoor
Step 1. If beginning indoors, transplant Cosmos outside once they are approximately 4 inches tall.

Condition 2: general
Step 1. Cosmos’ best growing conditions are hot and dry.

Step 2. To care for the Cosmos, be sure to deadhead spent blooms. This encourages more blooms to occur lower on the plant, which will make a stronger plant.

Condition 3: taller variaty
Step 1. Also, if you are growing a taller variety of Cosmos, you may need to stake the plant so it isn’t damaged by wind or grow the flowers against a fence. Another option is to plant the flowers closer than 2 feet to one another, where they will protect each other from the wind instead of staking.

Condition 5: general
Step 1. Cosmos grow nicer, larger flowers if they are watered regularly. Although Cosmos come in both annual and perennial varieties, the plant reseeds itself.

Condition 5: mix
Step 1. Cosmos do well when paired with cleome, dahlias and cannas.

Harvest Steps:

1. If hoping to harvest seeds or that the Cosmos will self-sow, leave a few flowers on the plant so they can turn into seed pods and reseed themselves.

Restriction

1. do not add fertilizer to the plants

2. If Cosmos are planted in an area with amended soil, the flowers grown there will be weak and will not stand upright.

Is this what you are expecting?

Brief Introduction:

Echinacae, more commonly known as coneflower, are care-free heavy bloomers that bloom from summer to fall. It is an easy flower to grow from seed, and it is known to be drought resistant and long lasting. They are commonly purple, but also can grow in white and yellow . Coneflower is a native to the eastern part of the United States.

Ready to go? Here is the instruction:

Purple Echinacea seeds available at ufseeds.com
White Swan Echinacea seeds available at ufseeds.com

Planting Steps:

1. Condition: general
Although coneflower plants are easy to care for, the seeds are tough to germinate. To plant coneflower from seed, take the seeds and sprinkle them on a damp paper towel that has been folded in half.

2. Condition: general
Fold the paper towel in half again to cover the seeds between the two damp halves.

3. Condition: general
Then, place the paper towel in a plastic bag, seal it and keep it in a refrigerator for eight to 12 weeks. This treatment copies winter conditions, which will allow the seeds to break dormancy to then germinate.

4. Condition: general
After the cold treatment, sow two coneflower seeds in 8-inch pots and cover with a small sprinkling of soil.

5. Condition: general
Cover the pots with a clear plastic bag but remove this once seedlings sprout so they can receive the full amount of light.

Growing Steps:

Condition 1: general
Step 1. Once coneflowers produce their second set of true leaves, transplant them outside into an area that receives full sun. Wait until after the last frost to do this.

Condition 2: normal rainfall
Step 1. During seasons where rainfall is normal, coneflowers do not require any additional watering.

Condition 3: dry seasons
Step 1. The coneflowers thrive in poor soil and are drought resistant, often thriving in dry seasons.

Condition 4: general
Step 1. Once the flowers die at the end of the season, cut the plant back by a third. This will encourage rejuvenation in the plant, and it may bloom a second time prior to the cold seasons.

Harvest Steps:

1. Harvest seeds by clipping the heads of the plant when they have died.

2. Pull off the petals and, while wearing gloves, rub the cone with your fingers to remove the seeds.

3. Put seeds in a paper bag in a dry area and leave the seeds until they dry. Once dry, store in a sealed bag until you are ready to go through the germination process.

Planting Steps:

1. Condition: general
Daisies are planted by seeds or by divisions.

2. Condition: general
To plant the seeds, loosen the soil and plant seeds 1/8 inch deep. The seeds can be planted even when a light frost may still occur. Seeds should germinate in up to 20 days.

3. Condition: seeding directly into the garden
Daisy flowers can be seeded directly into the garden but if this is done, don’t expect blooms until the following spring.

Growing Steps:

Condition 1: general
Step 1. Once established, Daisies require little care. Daisies grow well in clumps, and make sure they receive at least 1 inch of water per week.

Step 2. Add mulch to control weeds and retain moisture.

Condition 2: tall variaty
Step 1. Since Daisies can grow very tall, sometimes staking is necessary to prevent wind damage. Daisies also can support themselves if planted close together, or they can be planted along a fence row for more protection.

Condition 3: general
Step 1. To keep strong plants, divide the daisies every three to four years.

Step 2. To encourage the most growth, deadhead spent blooms.

Step 3. Once blooms finally fade, usually in mid-fall, cut the Daisies back to the ground.

Step 4. To prevent disease, do not overhead water the Daisies.

Harvest Steps:

1. Daisy seeds can be harvested, too. Wait for the flowers to dry out and, if harvesting the seeds, do not deadhead the blooms.

2. Cut the flowers and hang them upside down to dry. Seeds are kept in the Daisy heads.

3. Once the flowers have dried, crush the heads and remove the seeds.

Marigolds are a popular, long-lasting and brilliantly colored flowers. Marigolds are commonly grown as annuals, but some varieties are perennials. In addition to their fiery appearance, Marigolds also naturally repel mosquitos.

Planting Steps:

1. Condition: general
Begin Marigolds inside eight weeks before the last frost.

2. Condition: general
Fill a seed-starting tray with soilless potting mix and sprinkle the seeds over the surface.

3. Condition: general
Cover the seeds with a thin layer of vermiculture, then cover the tray with plastic and place in a warm area, such as the top of a refrigerator.

4. Condition: general
Marigolds do not require light to germinate. Marigolds seeds will sprout in as little as four days, and once seedlings appear, remove the plastic and move to a sunny location.

Growing Steps:

Condition 1: general
Step 1. Once seedlings have two true sets of leaves, move to their own pot and continue growing indoors until the last frost has passed.

Step 2. Transplant them outdoors in a sunny location. Since Marigolds are hardy, they grow well in moist or dry conditions.

Step 3. Encourage more growth by deadheading the spent blooms.

Condition 2: ground-planted
Step 1. If planted in the ground, only water the Marigolds if dry weather persists for more than two weeks.

Condition 3: container-planted
Step 1. Water Marigolds daily if they are growing in a container.

Harvest Steps:

1. harvest in the early morning when the blooms are just beginning to open.

2. Set the freshly cut stems in a vase with warm water.

3. After two hours, strip the lower leaves that are submerged in water. Marigolds will live a week in the vase if they are properly maintained.

4. To do so, cut the stem 1 inch from the ground on a sunny day.

5. Wash the entire stem and flower and only remove the stem immediately prior to serving so the flower has the best flavor.

6. Once deadheaded, the dead blooms can be kept in a dry, cool place and seeds can be harvested for the following year.

Restriction

1. Condition: perennials
If growing perennial Marigolds, do not fertilize in the winter months .

2. Condition: general
Be careful to not overfeed the Marigolds, as too much fertilizer will increase leaf production and decrease bloom production.

They are assumed to come in colors of red, but they also come in colors of white, pink and orange . Poppies come in both single and double blooms. Oftentimes, the red Poppy is used to symbolize fallen warriors and it commemorates Veteran’s Day.

Great Red Poppy seeds available at ufseeds.com

Planting Steps:

1. Condition: general
Poppies should be sown directly into the garden because they grow from a taproot, which makes the Poppy not a good transplant flower. If the taproot is disturbed during transplanting, the Poppy will die.

2. Condition: indoors
If you must start the Poppy seeds indoors, use peat pots that can be directly planted into the garden so the Poppy taproot isn’t disturbed.

3. Condition: outside
Direct sow the Poppy seeds outside in a poor to average soil. Barely cover the seeds, or leave them on the surface of the soil.

4. Condition: Different zones
If you live in Zones 3-7, direct sow seeds outdoors in the early spring when a frost may still occur. If you live in Zones 8-10, direct sow Poppy seeds in the fall.

Growing Steps:

Condition 1: general
Step 1. When the Poppies sprout, thin them to 6 inches apart.

Step 2. Once Poppies are established, they do not require frequent watering. Overwatering will result in leggy and tall Poppies.

Step 3. They do require deadheading and removing the spent blooms can encourage more blooms to grow.

Condition 2: limited spread
Step 1. Poppies self-sow, and if you don’t want them to spread and sprout in surprising places, pull the plants from the ground once they have finished blooming.

Harvest Steps:

1. Seedpods will form after the blooms die. Once the seeds can be shaken inside the pod, the pod is ready to clip off the plant.

2. Cut the pods open and spread the seeds out in a single layer and let dry for a week.

3. Once completely dry, store in an airtight container until ready to use for baking.

Restriction

1. Condition: with compost
Since compost is applied, mulch is not needed as the compost will prevent weeds and continuously provide nutrients to the Poppy plant.
